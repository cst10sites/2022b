Fishing Cat



The fishing cat is a medium-sized wild cat that catches fish in South and Southeast Asia.

The scientific name for the fishing cat is Prionailurus viverrinus.

Habitant

The fishing cat's natural range is unknown, but it is currently found across South and Southeast Asia. It mostly lives in the vicinity of wetlands, along river and streams, so it is well-adapted to catching fish in the water. It can also be seen in cultivated grasslands and agricultural areas with standing water.

Characteristics

Contrary to most domestic cat, the fishing cat is larger and stronger, and it has almost no aversion to water. One of its remarkable features is the layered structure of its fur, which is vital for it to adapt to life in the water. A layer of dense short hair lies next to the skin, so the water cannot penetrate it. In addition, it helps the fishing cat to keep warm in the water.

The fishing cat has deep yellowish-grey fur with black lines and spots. On its belly, the fur is lighter than on the back and sides. There are two stripes on its cheek, and two above the eyes running to the neck with broken lines on the forehead. The size of the stripes goes from narrow to broad. The fishing cat has short and rounded ears set low on the head. On the back of its ears bear a white spot. Unlike most domestic cats, the fishing cat's tail is less than half the length of head and body with several black rings at the end.
